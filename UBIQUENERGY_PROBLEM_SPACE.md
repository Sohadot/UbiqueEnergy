## The Vocabulary Problem

### Current Energy Vocabulary (20th Century Origin)

When professionals talk about energy systems, they use terms that embed outdated assumptions:

**Example 1: "Supply and Demand"**
- Assumes: Supply is centralized, demand is destination
- Reality: Supply is distributed, can come from anywhere
- Problem: "Supply and demand" creates mental model of linear flow
- Consequence: Policy based on this model fails when supply is distributed

**Example 2: "The Grid"**
- Assumes: One unified system with clear boundaries
- Reality: Multiple interconnected topologies with fuzzy boundaries
- Problem: "The grid" suggests monolithic architecture
- Consequence: Resilience planning assumes centralized control still works

**Example 3: "Renewable Energy"**
- Assumes: Renewable energy is a special category
- Reality: What matters is the topology of how energy moves, not its source
- Problem: "Renewable vs. fossil" creates false binary
- Consequence: Policy focuses on source rather than system structure

**Example 4: "Smart Grid"**
- Assumes: Adding sensors and control = intelligence
- Reality: Intelligence requires distributed decision-making, not just monitoring
- Problem: "Smart grid" is jargon without precision
- Consequence: Projects fail because "smart" is undefined

**Example 5: "Peak Demand"**
- Assumes: Demand is passive (happens to the system)
- Reality: Demand can be shaped and is becoming an active tool
- Problem: "Peak demand" implies static, unchangeable load
- Consequence: Planning ignores demand-side flexibility

### The Consequence

When professionals use outdated vocabulary to think about new problems, they:

1. **Misdiagnose problems** — See "supply shortage" when real problem is topology mismatch
2. **Propose wrong solutions** — Build more capacity when distribution needs restructuring
3. **Make costly mistakes** — Invest in centralized solutions when distributed solutions would work better
4. **Fail to innovate** — Can't imagine solutions because language limits thinking
5. **Create policy gridlock** — Regulations based on outdated models prevent innovation

**Real Example:**

California's rolling blackouts in 2020:

**Official narrative:** "We don't have enough supply."

**Reality:** The problem was topology:
- Solar supply peaked midday → demand low
- Solar dropped at 4-6pm → demand rising → grid unprepared
- Problem: Supply and demand were misaligned temporally and spatially
- Solution: Not "build more power plants" but "restructure topology to handle distributed, variable supply"

Using 20th-century vocabulary ("supply shortage") masked the real problem (temporal topology mismatch).

### What's Missing

Energy professionals have no unified framework to answer:

**Strategic Questions:**
- How do you design a grid that's stable with 80% variable renewable supply?
- How do distributed resources coordinate without central control?
- What makes a grid resilient vs. brittle?
- How do you plan for 20-year horizon when technology changes every 3 years?

**Technical Questions:**
- How do you evaluate different grid architectures?
- What are the tradeoffs between centralization and distribution?
- How do storage, demand response, and generation combine?
- How do you integrate high-speed communication with power flow?

**Policy Questions:**
- How should regulation change for distributed systems?
- What incentives drive resilience vs. efficiency?
- How do you balance local autonomy with system stability?
- What pricing models work for bidirectional energy?

**Investment Questions:**
- How do you value distributed resources?
- What business models work for topology-based systems?
- How do you evaluate long-term resilience vs. short-term returns?
- Where should capital flow?

### What Currently Exists

**Fragmented Solutions:**

1. **Academic Papers** (Good for theory, hard to apply)
   - Scattered across journals
   - Use different terminology
   - No unified framework
   - Often overly mathematical

2. **Vendor Documentation** (Biased toward their solution)
   - Smart meter companies describe "smart metering"
   - Battery companies describe "storage solutions"
   - Software vendors describe "grid optimization"
   - Each ignores problems outside their domain

3. **Standards & Regulations** (Rule-based, not principle-based)
   - IEC 61850 (communication standard)
   - NERC CIP (security standard)
   - NIST Framework (governance framework)
   - None provide conceptual foundation

4. **Energy Company Internal Knowledge** (Proprietary, not shared)
   - Tesla knows distributed inverter coordination
   - Siemens understands grid automation
   - Utilities have operational experience
   - Knowledge is locked inside organizations

5. **News & Opinion** (Lacks rigor)
   - Articles about "the clean energy transition"
   - Blog posts about "the future of energy"
   - Think tank reports with policy recommendations
   - Missing: foundational thinking

### The Result

An engineer designing a microgrid must:
- Read academic papers (too theoretical)
- Study vendor specs (too narrow)
- Follow regulatory standards (too restrictive)
- Guess at principles (no framework)

There is **no coherent reference** that:
- Provides principled framework
- Applies to multiple scales
- Balances theory and practice
- Is institutionally credible
- Can be cited and built upon

## Why This Gap Exists

### Structural Reasons

**1. Fragmentation of Knowledge**
- Energy is split across: power systems, communications, controls, economics, policy
- Each domain has its own vocabulary
- No unified language exists
- Creating unified language requires work across all domains

**2. Commercial Incentives Misaligned**
- Vendors benefit from fragmentation (their product is "the solution")
- Utilities benefit from keeping knowledge internal (competitive advantage)
- Consultancies benefit from custom solutions (not generalizable frameworks)
- No one is incentivized to create open reference framework

**3. Regulation Lags Reality**
- Regulations are written based on current practice
- By the time regulations exist, practice has moved on
- Regulation is codified slowly, evolved slowly
- Creates institutional inertia

**4. Speed of Change Exceeds Consensus Building**
- Creating standards requires institutional consensus (slow)
- Technology changes faster than consensus (1-2 years vs. 5-7 years)
- By the time consensus is reached, new problems exist

**5. No Natural Institutional Owner**
- Universities research but don't maintain operational frameworks
- Utilities focus on their own system, not general frameworks
- Vendors benefit from confusion
- Government moves slowly
- Industry associations are consensus-based (slow)

**Result:** The gap remains unfilled because no institution is structurally incentivized to fill it.
